Students participating in DLMOOC

Posted on January 6, 2014 by Karen Fasimpaur
1

This is a guest post from Alec Patton, a Humanities teacher at High Tech High North County.

In only a few weeks, my 11th grade honors humanities students will be taking part in DLMOOC 2014. Honors happens in an unusual way in High Tech High schools  - since we’re totally non-streaming, honors students attend the same humanities class as everyone else, they just do extra work. For my students, this means extra reading, and extra essay assignments. (You can see all the fall’s honors assignments on my digital portfolio here, if you’re interested.)

I have always based the essay assignments on articles that I found interesting, and this fall (mostly by accident) these articles were all about equity in education: we started with a New York Times article about the impact “stereotype threat” on students, then followed with “The Examined Life”, Malcolm Gladwell’s brief history of aptitude tests. We concluded with British journalist Gary Younge’s account of a 2003 Supreme Court challenge to affirmative action in university admissions. At the same time, we read Tobias Wolff’s Old School, which is about a Jewish student attending an elite New England prep school in the early 1960s. As you might imagine, the students had a lot to say about equity in education, and I was able to learn a great deal from them.

So DLMOOC sounds like a perfect fit for my honors class. My honors students and I (as well as any other student who would like to join) will be enrolling in the course. I hope that all of you will be able to learn as much from them as I have!

The joys of open and online

Posted on December 31, 2013 by Karen Fasimpaur
Reply

As the time for the start of DLMOOC draws nearer (T minus 20 days!), registration is growing, and we now have over 1,300 participants.

As I look at the registered participants, I am reminded of one of the delights of open and online: we have a diverse group of interesting folks participating from around the world. Here are some quick stats.

  • We have participants in 66 countries.

  • Thirty-nine percent of our participants are from outside the United States.
  • The primary role in education cited by most participants is teacher (40%). We also have administrators (19%), instructional coaches (8%), university faculty (5%), students (K-12 and university), parents, and others.

We know that all this diversity is going to lead to one interesting MOOC!

Experiments, lectures, and panel discussions (oh my)

Posted on December 13, 2013 by Ben Daley
Reply

We’re viewing this MOOC as an experiment: Can we create a deeper learning experience for a large number of people around the world in an online environment? One problem with “traditional” MOOCs is that they largely replicate the outdated “sage on the stage” model of teaching. One aspect of the course that we are going to experiment with is having a panel discussion. Now, a panel discussion (whether in person or online) can just be a series of lectures. When I went to AERA last year, I thought, “you know, four bad 15-minute lectures is still really a bad one hour lecture.” So, panel discussions are no panacea.

One image I hold in my mind is “lean back or lean forward?” We don’t want participants (and k12 students) leaning back like they are at the movies. We want them leaning forward (dare I say “leaning in”) and actively participating. So, when we broadcast a panel discussion, we want people to be able to interact with the panelists and one another so that participants are processing the conversation through dialogue and making their own meaning of the material. Not just passively receiving information.

DLMOOC Preview – Put It Into Practice

Posted on November 6, 2013 by Karen Fasimpaur
Reply

The DLMOOC Put it Into Practice (PIP) activities are a way for you to explore what our topics in more depth with students or colleagues. We’d also invite you to share what you did and how it went with a small group at your site or on our G+ Community.

In this week’s activity, you and/or your students or colleagues will have a chance to assess your own mindset and think more about how academic mindsets affect our learning.

  1. Take of one of these academic mindset assessments (You might also have your students or colleagues take one and then debrief as a group):

  • Test your mindset from Carol Dweck
  • What’s my mindset? from Mindset Works (ages 12 and up)
  • What’s my classroom mindset? from Mindset Works (educator oriented)
  • What’s my school’s mindset? from Mindset Works (educator oriented)
  • The Mindset Self- Assessment from Global Innovations (career oriented)

Or if you’d prefer, try developing your mindset survey and post it to the G+ Community. SurveyMonkey or Google doc forms are good tools for something like this.

  1. Look at the results. Do you mostly have a fixed or growth mindset? Does this seem right to you? How do you think this affects your learning process and how you approach things in your daily life?

  2. Post a short comment on the G+ Community about this exercise and what you learned from it in the context of our discussions this week, and/or tweet it out as well. Don’t forget our hashtags: #dlmooc and #deeperlearning.
